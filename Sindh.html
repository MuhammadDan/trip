SINDH

Pakistan consist of four provinces.Its second largest province is Sindh, with its capital in Karachi, which is not only the most populous metropolis of the country but also a commercial hub.It has two gigantic seaport located in Karachi. Some famous places of Sindh are as follows:

  • SHRINE OF LAL SHAHBAZ QALANDAR
  • MAZAR E QUAID
  • MOHENJO DARO

SHRINE OF LAL SHAHBAZ QALANDAR

The Shrine of Lal Shabaz Qalandar is a shrine and mausoleum dedicated to the 13th century Muslim and Sufi saint, Lal Shahbaz Qalandar. The shrine is located in Sehwan Sharif, in the Pakistani province of Sindh. The shrine is one of the most important in Pakistan, and attracts up to one million visitors annually.

MAZAR E QUAID

Mohammed Ali Jinnah was the founder and first governor-general (1947-48) of Pakistan. He is revered as the father of Pakistan. Mazar-e-Quaid, also known as the Jinnah Mausoleum, is a national monument in Karachi, Pakistan.It is the final resting place of Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the founder of Pakistan. The mausoleum was designed by Pakistani architect Yahya Merchant and completed in 1960.It is located in the center of a large park known as the Mazar-e-Quaid Garden. The mausoleum is made of white marble and features a large central dome surrounded by four smaller domes.The interior of the mausoleum is decorated with intricate geometric patterns and marble inlays. Visitors to the mausoleum can pay their respects to Jinnah and learn more about his life and contributions to Pakistan.The mausoleum is a popular tourist destination and is considered a symbol of national unity. It is also the site of various national ceremonies and events.The Mazar-e-Quaid is an important cultural and historical landmark in Pakistan.

MOHENJO DARO

Mohenjo Daro was an ancient city located in the Sindh province of modern-day Pakistan. It is believed to have been founded around 2500 BCE and flourished during the Indus Valley Civilization.Mohenjo Daro was an important trade center and is known for its well-planned streets, elaborate drainage systems, and advanced brick-making technology. The city was also home to a number of large public buildings, including a Great Bath, a large assembly hall, and several granaries.The Indus Valley Civilization, of which Mohenjo Daro was a part, is notable for its advanced level of urbanization, as well as its use of standardized weights and measures, which suggest a sophisticated system of trade and governance.Mohenjo Daro is thought to have been abandoned around 1900 BCE, although the exact reason for its decline is not known. Some theories suggest that the city may have been abandoned due to a change in trade routes, while others suggest that it may have been abandoned due to environmental factors, such as a change in the course of the Indus River.Mohenjo Daro was rediscovered in the 1920s by a team of archaeologists led by John Marshall, and excavations of the site have continued to the present day. The city is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is a popular tourist attraction in Pakistan.
